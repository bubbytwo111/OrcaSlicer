Beginner Quick Start

ROCK3TS 3D for a clean, confident first print

This quick start is built for beginners. It keeps you focused on the settings that matter, avoids the risky ones, and gives you a simple path from opening a model to exporting a safe first file.

1

Open your model

Click Open or drag in your model file. As soon as it appears, check the size, position, and orientation on the build plate.

  • If the model looks too large, too small, or partly off the bed, stop and check placement before slicing.
  • Use arrange and move tools carefully. Do not rush through placement.
2

Confirm the printer preset

The printer preset controls the size and movement assumptions of the slicer. This must match your real machine.

  • Confirm the printer model.
  • Confirm the nozzle size.
  • If you are unsure, do not guess. Go back to a known-good preset first.
3

Match the filament preset

Use a filament preset that matches the actual material loaded in the machine so temperature and flow assumptions stay sensible.

  • PLA should use a PLA preset.
  • PETG should use a PETG preset.
  • Do not mix material types unless you know exactly why.
4

Slice and inspect the preview

Click Slice and inspect the preview before exporting anything. The preview is where you catch mistakes early.

  • Look for strange gaps, missing walls, bad supports, or weird travel patterns.
  • If something looks wrong, do not export yet. Re-check presets and placement first.
5

Export and watch the first layer

Once the preview looks right, export the file and stay with the printer at the beginning of the print.

  • Stop immediately if the nozzle drags, the print starts in the wrong place, or the filament does not stick.
  • A good first layer matters more than finishing a bad print.

What beginners should avoid

Leave start G-code, end G-code, machine limits, bed size, nozzle diameter, acceleration, and firmware-specific settings alone until you are comfortable. Those options can change real printer behavior in ways that are much harder to recover from than a simple slicer mistake.
